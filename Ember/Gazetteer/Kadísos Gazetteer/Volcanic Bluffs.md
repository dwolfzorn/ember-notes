# Volcanic Bluffs

**Work In Progress!** This biome and its accompanying entry are not in the scope of Alpha One Gameplay. All text provided here is currently **in development** and **incomplete**. This entry will be revised and expanded on in future updates.

The windswept rocky bluffs of Kadisos serve as a transitional zone, connecting the lush jungles to the towering mountains or the vast oceans that surround the island. These bluffs can be found along the entire perimeter of the island, with the exception of the southern edge where they give way to the enchanting Tidal Pools.

### Environment

The bluffs are characterized by their rugged terrain and exposed rock formations. The strong ocean winds constantly batter against these cliffs, shaping them into majestic and imposing structures. It is a sight to behold as waves crash against the rocks, creating a mesmerizing display of spray and foam.

### Ecology

Tall grasses, bushes and rocky outcrops are common in this biome, with large areas of open space dominated by unchecked growth. While hardier trees and palms can also be found scattered across the areas, they think out quickly as one leaves the edge of the jungle.

The flora of the bluffs is hardy and resilient, adapted to withstand the strong winds and rocky terrain. Tall grasses sway in the breeze, providing cover for small animals and insects. Hardy bushes cling to the cliffsides, their roots anchored deep into crevices in the rocks. Some of these bushes bear small, brightly colored berries that serve as a food source for birds and other creatures but not fit for people.

The most common predators in this biome are the stealthy crag raptors and agile kadhana lizards, both of whom use the bluffs for nesting and as their hunting grounds. Both rely on their superior mobility to reach prey that think they are safe. Their keen eyesight and ability to blend into the terrain let them watch for prey, while the climbing kadhana make easy meals of nests situated in sheer cliffs overlooking the sea.

### Lore

Not much is known about the history of the bluffs, though much of their volcanic activity has ceased, they are named for the obvious hallmarks of that activity which formed them. Overall, the bluffs offer a unique ecosystem where both predator and prey coexist in a delicate balance between rock and sky, ocean and jungle.
