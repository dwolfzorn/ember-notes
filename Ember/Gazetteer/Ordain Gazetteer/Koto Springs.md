# Koto Springs

- **District Type:** Residential and Commercial
- **Populations:** 2,590 (Low-Moderate. Koto Springs features many wealthy private homes built around spas, inns, clubs, and shops. The district is thick with foot traffic at all hours.)
- **Lifestyle:** Mixed. (Nobles and merchants life here, but all of Ordain visits to enjoy the springs.)
- **Services:** Bathhouses, hospitality, fine dining.

Koto Springs flourished around its namesake natural springs — deep, ancient wells of hot water that bubble up through stone and earth. The city’s finest engineers have tamed these waters, weaving networks of aqueducts and copper piping across streets and courtyards. Here, residents enjoy private steam baths and indoor plumbing that not even the noble manors elsewhere in the city can rival.

### Culture

Life in Koto Springs centers around public bathing and leisurely socializing, whether with friends or with clients. Business is often negotiated in the mosaic-tiled saunas of bathhouses or in vibrant private gardens draped in shade and greenery.

While some springs feed exclusive clubs or secretive spas, others are open to travelers and commoners who come to soak away troubles and gather news among the restorative waters.

Service trades — masseurs, cooks, cleaners, plumbers — are held in high esteem here as they are often in demand. Though outsiders flock to the springs, the long-settled families who maintain the pipes and operate the oldest inns are respected as the district’s heart.

### Landmarks

**Koto Springs Bathhouse**

Ornate columns and flowering hedges mark the entrance to the oldest and most famous bathouse in the city. Here the polished marble floors and mineral pools host the city’s most influential figures. Cost to enter is high, but the quality is second to none.

**The Copper Cauldron**

This bustling café, perched atop a steaming fissure, prepares its vegetarian fare using ingenious steam-powered ovens and spits, filling the air with the scents of roasted herbs and bubbling broths. The Copper Cauldron is an expensive but well-loved eatery in the district.

**The Vapor Room**

A luxurious, lantern-lit social club and sauna where the well-to-do mingle in privacy, relaxing in crystal-tiled steam baths. The Vapor Room greedily scoops up all the second-rung clients that aren't important or rich enough to get into the famed Koto Springs Bathhouse.

**Gajak's Rest**

Built shortly after the district's namesake, Gajak's Rest is the oldest inn the district has, and is a sprawling collection of buildings with a tavern, multiple inn buildings, stables, and its own small restaurant. Prices are modest, and service is good here.

### Locals

**Rilano Mestrin (Lawful Neutral, Ordani Human, he/him)**

Chief steward of the Koto Springs Bathhouse, Rilano is known for his dignified manner and frightening, encyclopedic memory for guests’ preferences and secrets. He has connections with merchants, vendors, and Trading House officials throughout the city, and uses them to keep the bathhouse running smoothly.

**Parne Valitaine (Chaotic Good, Tayan Human, she/her)**

The bright and chipper proprietor of the Copper Cauldron, Parne’s an expatriate of the Tayan Empire who left to explore Ember's culinary landscape. While she's presently settled in Koto Springs, she has plans to move on eventually.

**Rocklin Darix (Neutral, Ordani Hulg'run, they/them)**

This patient, nearly silent caretaker tends the public moss gardens and pipes around the district with a backpack of tools and spare materials. Taciturn and more interested in their work that chatting, they have lived in Koto Springs for as long as anyone can remember. They have a standing agreement with district leadership to do their work.

**Festra Yod (Lawful Good, Ordani Human, she/her)**

The matronly hostess of Gajak’s Rest and its current owner. She is the heart and soul of the inn, and is a highly respected fixture of the district after decades in the business. Festra wields kindness and hot meals like a fencer does a rapier.

## Lore

Although many believe the district was named after its famed bathhouse, the truth is the reverse: the springs and their first great bath preceded the neighborhood, which slowly grew into a hub for both visitors and those seeking the springs’ rumored healing powers.

Whether the waters truly cure aches is a subject of friendly debate, but the legend itself is actively perpetuated by numerous merchants and bath owners that want to maintain the air of mystique around their venues.
